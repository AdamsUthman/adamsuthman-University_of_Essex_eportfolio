UNIT 11: POINTERS, REFERENCES & MEMORY, AND DESIGN PATTERNS

With the aid of design proposals created in unit 7 using UML diagrams and object-oriented programming, unit 11 comprises the construction of the assignment of driverless automobile situations. This unit also addresses Python's lack of pointers and their significance for memory organization. This section also explains design patterns in object-oriented programs and the distinctions between creational, structural, and behavioral versions. Finally, the idea of sustainable development is defined, along with the contribution of object-oriented programming to sustainability.

In this unit, we read an intriguing paper (Guo, 2021) regarding software development for academic purposes (i.e., Python Tutor). There are many insightful recommendations in the paper that can be used for small-budget or personal projects, including "Just make it work," "Keep it simple," "Reject most user suggestions" (which isn't as bad as it sounds), "Be stateless," "Sharing, not hosting," "Minimize dependencies," and "Use old technologies." I suppose the author also offers a road map for individual or small projects, or at the very least for the early stages of a potentially large project, even though he makes clear proposals for software development efforts in academia.

SKILLS/KNOWLEDGE GAINED

  • During this unit, I learnt that every python object contains at least three pieces of data namely: References count, Type, Value.
  • I also learnt that in python, when you create a variable, a python object is created with given data(type, value and the reference count), and the name is assigned (bond) to this object.
  • I also learnt that design patterns are broken down into three categories namely: Pattern, Associated problem, Solution, Consequences.
  • I learnt that python doesn't have pointers like C does.

REFERENCES

Guo, P. (2021) Ten Million Users and Ten Years Later: Python Tutor’s Design Guidelines for Building Scalable and Sustainable Research Software in Academia.
